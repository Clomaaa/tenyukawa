Interpretation:
Changes in Temperature since 1880

    The world is getting warmer. Thermometer readings around the world have been rising since the Industrial Revolution, and the causes are a blend of human activity and some natural variability—with the preponderance of evidence saying humans are mostly responsible.

    Over the past decades, there has been a clear gap of increase in global temperature comparing the years 1880 and 2020, indicating that the highest recorded global temperature was in 2020, which rose at 14.88 °C.

    The average temperature from 1880 to 1910 did not clearly show an increase in temperature as the temperature from 1890 decreased until 1910, with an average of 13.7 °C among these four decades. However, from the 1920s to 1940s, the temperature climbed up gradually by 0.1 °C, showing an increase which then stabilized at about 14°C until the 1980s and is still getting hotter until 2020.

    These observed changes indicated that the rise in average global temperatures was not stable from the 1880s to 2020, as certain decades have either lower or higher average temperatures than the preceding one.

    Since 1880, the Earth's temperature has risen by 0.14° F (0.08° C) every decade, and the rate of warming over the last 40 years has been more than double that: 0.32° F (0.18° C) per decade since 1981. Utilizing the 0.18°C increase rate from 1980 to 2020, we can use the explicit calculation "an = 0.18(n) + 14" where n is the nth term of the sequence to achieve a reasonable approximation of the future world average temperatures. Reminder, the first term of the sequence should be the Global Average Temperature for the decade of 1980.

    This gradual increase in average temperature may not be relatively high to affect the population only, but given the enormous size and heat capacity of the world's oceans, even a slight increase in the average annual temperature requires a large quantity of heat energy. Although the increase in world average temperature may appear little, it represents a huge increase in collected heat, which significantly affects climate change.
